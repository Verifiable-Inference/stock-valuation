What This Tool Does

This tool computes the intrinsic (fair) value of a stock using a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model. The core idea: a business is worth the sum of all the cash it will generate in the future, adjusted for the fact that a dollar received ten years from now is worth less than a dollar today.

Data comes from two sources: SEC EDGAR for up to 10 years of audited annual 10-K financials, and Yahoo Finance for live price, beta, market cap, and the 10-year Treasury yield. The output is a fair-value estimate per share that you can compare against the current market price.

What it does not do: predict stock prices, account for sentiment or momentum, or value financial companies (banks, insurers) — those require fundamentally different models.

How It Works

  1. Data pull — 10 years of annual 10-K filings from SEC EDGAR, plus live price, beta, market cap, and the 10-year Treasury yield from Yahoo Finance.
  2. FCF history — Computes unlevered free cash flow for each historical year: EBIT × (1 − tax) + D&A − CapEx − ΔNWC. Falls back to OCF − CapEx when EBIT is unavailable.
  3. WACC — Builds the discount rate from first principles: CAPM cost of equity + after-tax cost of debt, weighted by the company's actual capital structure.
  4. Projection — Seeds growth from the historical FCF CAGR, clamps it to a sane band, then linearly fades it toward the terminal growth rate over the forecast horizon.
  5. DCF — Discounts each projected year's FCF to present value, adds a Gordon-growth terminal value, subtracts net debt, divides by shares → intrinsic value per share.
  6. Sensitivity — Re-runs the DCF across 25 combinations of WACC and terminal growth to show how the answer shifts under different assumptions.
  7. Report — Renders a self-contained HTML page with all charts and intermediate figures.
PV = Σ FCFt / (1+r)t + TV / (1+r)n

where r = WACC, n = forecast horizon, TV = FCFn×(1+g) / (WACC−g)

Assumptions

Every assumption below has a default baked into config.yaml. All of them can be overridden in the Assumptions panel on the form, on the command line, or per-ticker in the overrides block of config.yaml.

Forecast Years (n)

Default: 5

The number of years for which cash flows are projected explicitly before collapsing into a terminal value. Five years is the standard in equity research — short enough that projections stay grounded in current trends, long enough to capture a meaningful fraction of value before the terminal value dominates.

When to change it: Use 7–10 years for companies where the investment thesis plays out slowly (e.g., infrastructure, drug pipelines). Use 3 years when near-term visibility is poor or the business is in rapid transition.

Terminal Growth Rate

Default: 2.5% Most impactful assumption

The assumed perpetual growth rate of free cash flow once the explicit forecast ends. This is the single most impactful assumption in any DCF — small changes here move the fair value significantly (as the sensitivity heatmap makes visible).

The default of 2.5% approximates long-run nominal GDP growth. It is derived from a conservative blend: roughly 2% real GDP growth + a modest inflation contribution, applied to FCF (which tends to grow more slowly than revenue or GDP at the firm level for mature businesses).

Rule of thumb: No company can grow faster than the overall economy forever — if terminal growth exceeds GDP growth (~4% nominal historically), the company would eventually become larger than the economy itself. Keep this between 1.5% and 3.5% for most businesses. The model will refuse to run if terminal growth meets or exceeds WACC (the Gordon formula becomes undefined).

Equity Risk Premium (ERP)

Default: 5.0%

The additional return investors demand for holding equities versus a risk-free Treasury bond. It enters the CAPM formula as:

Cost of Equity = Risk-free rate + Beta × ERP

The 5.0% default is the long-run historical equity risk premium for US equities, consistent with Damodaran's widely-cited estimates. It is slightly conservative relative to current implied ERP (which fluctuates with market valuations) but robust over longer holding periods.

Raising this to 5.5–6% increases the cost of equity and lowers fair value. Lowering it to 4% does the opposite. In high-rate environments the implied ERP tends to compress (since bonds become more competitive), warranting a lower value here.

Risk-Free Rate

Auto: live 10-yr Treasury (^TNX)

The return on a theoretically risk-free investment — the baseline in CAPM. The tool pulls the current 10-year US Treasury yield live from Yahoo Finance at run time, so it always reflects prevailing interest rates without manual input.

Override with --risk-free 0.043 to freeze a specific rate for comparability across runs. If the live fetch fails, the tool falls back to 4.0% and logs a warning.

Beta

Auto: 5-yr monthly vs. S&P 500 Fallback: 1.0

Beta measures how much a stock moves relative to the overall market. A beta of 1.0 means it tracks the market; 1.5 means 50% more volatile. Higher beta → higher cost of equity → higher WACC → lower intrinsic value.

The tool fetches the 5-year monthly regression beta from Yahoo Finance, the most common convention in equity research. Override with --beta 1.2 when historical beta is unrepresentative — e.g., after a major restructuring, a spinoff, or when you prefer an industry-average unlevered beta.

Fallback: 1.0 (market-average risk) when data is unavailable.

WACC Override

Default: null (auto-computed)

Bypasses the entire CAPM-based WACC calculation and forces a fixed discount rate. The auto-computed WACC is grounded in observed market data and should be preferred.

Use this sparingly — mainly to match a specific analyst's model or apply an industry-standard rate for comparability. Example: --wacc 0.09 forces a 9% discount rate regardless of beta, leverage, or prevailing rates.

Tax Rate

Auto: computed from filings Fallback: 21%

The effective corporate tax rate applied to EBIT to convert operating income into after-tax cash flow (NOPAT). The tool computes this from actual filings: Income Tax Expense ÷ Pretax Income averaged across all profitable historical years, smoothing year-to-year volatility.

This is more accurate than the US statutory 21% rate because it captures actual deductions, credits, and international tax structures. The 21% fallback (the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 US corporate statutory rate) applies only when tax data is missing from filings.

Override with --tax-rate 0.15 for companies with structural tax advantages (e.g., heavy R&D credits, IP held in low-tax jurisdictions).

Base FCF Method

Default: trailing

Controls how the starting point for projections is estimated. Two options:

  • Trailing (default): 3-year trailing average of actual FCF. Works well for stable, predictable businesses.
  • Normalized: Designed for cyclical companies (semiconductor memory, commodities, oil & gas). Computes the average FCF margin (FCF ÷ Revenue) across the full history — capturing mid-cycle profitability — then applies that margin to the most recent revenue figure. This avoids anchoring the model on a boom or bust year. Use for names like MU, FCX, or CVX.

Base FCF Averaging Window

Default: 3 years

When using the trailing method, the tool averages the last N years of FCF rather than anchoring on a single year. This dampens distortions from lumpy CapEx cycles, one-time working-capital swings, or acquisitions.

Set to 1 to use only the most recent year — appropriate when the latest year is genuinely the best representation of run-rate earnings (e.g., after a major business transformation or divestiture).

Initial FCF Growth Rate

Auto: historical FCF CAGR

The first-year growth rate seeding the projection. By default it is computed as the compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of historical FCF, clamped to the guardrail band. Growth is then linearly faded to the terminal growth rate across the forecast horizon — the model never assumes the current growth rate lasts forever.

Override with --growth 0.10 when the historical CAGR is an unreliable signal — e.g., a large acquisition makes the FCF history non-comparable, or consensus analyst estimates are materially different from the historical trend.

For normalized cyclical companies, growth is seeded from the revenue CAGR instead (steadier than the FCF CAGR), assuming the mid-cycle margin holds.

Growth Guardrails

Max: 15% Min: −5%

Hard caps applied to the auto-derived initial growth rate regardless of what the historical CAGR implies.

  • 15% ceiling: Prevents a company with a great recent run from being projected at an unsustainable pace. Even elite compounders rarely sustain >15% FCF growth long enough to be the right base assumption.
  • −5% floor: Prevents a temporary FCF dip (heavy investment year, recession trough) from projecting permanent decline. If the business is genuinely in structural decline, override growth manually.

History Years

Default: 10

How many years of annual filings to pull from EDGAR when computing historical trends. Ten years captures at least one full economic cycle — important for a stable FCF CAGR and tax rate estimate. SEC XBRL data typically goes back to 2009, making 10 years effectively the practical maximum for most companies.

Fallback Values (when data is missing)

  • Risk-free rate: 4.0% (logged as a warning)
  • Beta: 1.0 (market-average)
  • Tax rate: 21% (US statutory)
  • Cost of debt: 5.0% (solid investment-grade rate)

Fallbacks are always logged in the report's Notes section so you know when they've been applied.

Charts

The report includes up to six charts. Here is what each shows and how to read it.

1 · Historical Revenue & Free Cash Flow

What it shows: Blue bars are annual revenue. The green line (with dots) is free cash flow — both in billions of dollars, going back up to 10 years.

How to read it: Revenue tells you how fast the company has been growing its top line. The FCF line tells you how much of that revenue translated into actual cash for shareholders after all operating costs, taxes, and reinvestment. A widening gap where FCF grows faster than revenue signals improving capital efficiency. A flat or declining FCF line against growing revenue signals the company is consuming cash to grow. This chart is the foundation — the model's projections are grounded in this history.

2 · Projected FCF vs. Present Value

What it shows: Two overlapping bars for each forecast year. Taller blue bars are raw projected FCF in each year ($B). Narrower green bars are what those same cash flows are worth today — discounted back to the present using WACC.

How to read it: This makes the time-value of money visible. A dollar of FCF in Year 5 is worth less than a dollar today because of risk and opportunity cost — that's what WACC captures. The shrinking green bars show exactly how much discounting erodes distant cash flows. If the green bars in Years 4–5 are very small relative to Year 1, the valuation is dominated by near-term earnings and the terminal value, not the mid-period growth story.

3 · Enterprise Value → Equity Value Bridge

What it shows: A waterfall chart: Enterprise Value → minus Debt → plus Cash & Investments → Equity Value. All in $B.

How to read it: Enterprise Value is what the entire business is worth as if you bought it free of any financing. But shareholders don't own it free and clear — debt holders have a prior claim. Subtracting debt and adding back cash gives the equity value: what's left for shareholders. Dividing by shares gives the per-share intrinsic value. This chart makes it transparent whether a large spread between enterprise value and equity value is due to heavy leverage or simply a large cash hoard.

4 · Sensitivity Analysis Heatmap

What it shows: A 5×5 grid where each cell is an intrinsic value per share. The Y-axis varies WACC (±2% around the base case). The X-axis varies terminal growth (±1% around the base case). Green = higher value; red = lower value.

How to read it: No DCF assumption is known with certainty. This chart is the honest answer to "how confident are you?" The center cell is the base-case estimate. If the current stock price sits comfortably in the green zone even in the top-left corner (high WACC, low growth), the stock looks cheap under almost any reasonable assumption. If it only looks attractive in the bottom-right corner (low WACC, high growth), the bull case depends on everything going right.

A smart use of this chart: mark where the current market price falls on the color scale. That reveals what assumptions the market is implicitly pricing in.

5 · Price vs. Intrinsic Value

What it shows: Two bars side by side — current market price per share (gray) and the model's intrinsic value per share (green if undervalued, red if overvalued). Percentage upside or downside is shown in the title.

How to read it: This is the bottom line. The model says the stock is worth $X; the market is paying $Y. Green bar taller than gray = the model sees an undervalued stock. Red bar shorter than gray = the market is pricing in more optimism than the model's assumptions justify. Always cross-reference with the sensitivity heatmap to understand how robust the conclusion is.

6 · Comparison Chart (multi-ticker runs only)

What it shows: A horizontal bar chart ranking all valued tickers by implied upside/downside vs. their current market price. Bars to the right (green) are undervalued; bars to the left (red) are overvalued. Tickers that could not be valued are listed separately.

How to read it: A relative ranking tool. It answers "across the stocks I'm considering, which does this model flag as most attractively priced?" Useful for portfolio construction or capital allocation decisions. Important caveat: stocks with very high implied upside often carry a higher WACC (more risk), not necessarily a better business. Always read the upside figure alongside the individual company's assumptions.

When Not to Trust It

Limitations & Edge Cases

  • FCF-negative companies cannot be valued with this model (early-stage, deeply cyclical at a trough, or distressed businesses). The tool will say so explicitly rather than produce a misleading negative number.
  • Financial companies (banks, insurers, brokers — SIC 6000–6499) are excluded. They have no meaningful distinction between operating and financing cash flows, making FCFF-based DCF inapplicable.
  • Foreign filers are supported via IFRS 20-F filings, but IFRS tagging is less standardized and figures may be noisier. All amounts are converted to USD using the current spot FX rate — a simplification that preserves growth rates but rescales absolute levels.
  • Terminal value dominates: In a 5-year DCF, the terminal value typically represents 60–80% of total enterprise value. This means terminal growth rate changes have an outsized effect on fair value — use the sensitivity chart to understand this exposure before drawing conclusions.
  • yfinance is unofficial — Yahoo Finance data (beta, price, market cap) is crowd-sourced and may lag or contain errors. Verify key figures before relying on them.
